Additional Information

(only for those really interested in procrastination!!)

Procrastination, like all things to do with humans, starts with our brains.
According to Ness Labs, procrastination is caused by the limbic system overriding
our prefrontal cortex. The limbic system, more commonly known as the paleomammalian
brain, is one of the oldest and most dominant parts of the brain, responsible for
deciding your emotions and instinctual decisions to stimuli, such as the fight or
flight response. This was valuable for prehistoric humans, as short-term decisions
to escape from danger were valuable. However the limbic system doesn't consider the
long term. This is where our prefrontal cortex comes in, as the prefrontal cortex is
responsible for complex behaviour and making long term decisions. However due to being
a newer part of the brain, it is less dominant compared to the limbic system and as such
decisions which benefit you in the short term is the decision you are more likely to make
due to the limbic system overriding other parts of the brain especially the prefrontal
cortex, which leads to procrastination.

So to stop procrastination, we need to understand how our brain reacts to
tasks, both emotionally and habitually. According to Harvard Business Review,
procrastination is caused by 3 main factors.

1. Poor discipline, arising from bad habits
2. Intolerance of negative emotions we think will occur due to doing the work, such as anxiety and boredom
3. Common brain patterns, such as underestimating/overestimating the amount of work needed to complete a task, and cognitive blocks

Fixing these cognitive problems in our lives can significantly improve the amount
of work we can get done, and therefore make us feel more productive and therefore
more accomplished and happy.

There is lots of advice on how to avoid procrastination on the internet, but the
predominant theme in all advice is about forming consistency, or a habit. As if you
just avoid procrastination for a week, and then become lax and go back to your old
behaviour, nothing will change. As such, forming a habit to prevent wanting to
back to procrastination.

According to a study done, on average it takes 66 days for a behaviour to become
automatic, i.e. it takes 66 days to form a habit. So our goal should be to replace
our procrastination habit with a working habit. This is quite a challenging goal, as
to wean oneself off procrastination we need to change how we perceive work, something
which is nearly always a challenge and brings negative emotions and/or a lack of positive emotions.

Some ways to start working your way through procrastination can be breaking down your large
objectives into smaller, easier goals, starting off with the easier parts of the project to
get into the rhythm of work, and clear possible distractions. The following video summarises
